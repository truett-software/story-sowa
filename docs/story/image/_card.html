• Aristotle

    Aristotle was a Greek philosopher. His writings span the natural sciences, philosophy, linguistics, economics, politics, psychology and the arts. The Aristotelian tradition set the groundwork for the development of modern science. With the Prior Analytics, Aristotle is credited with the earliest study of formal logic, and his conception of it was the dominant form of Western logic until 19th-century advances in mathematical logic.

  • Leonard Euler

    Leonhard Euler was a Swiss mathematician, physicist, astronomer, geographer, logician and engineer who founded the studies of graph theory and topology. In 1735, Euler presented a solution to the problem known as the Seven Bridges of KÃ¶nigsberg. The problem is to decide whether it is possible to follow a path that crosses each bridge exactly once and returns to the starting point. It is not possible: there is no path in a finite graph that visits every edge exactly once. This solution is considered to be the first theorem of graph theory.

  • Charles Sanders Peirce

    Charles Sanders Peirce was an American philosopher, logician, mathematician and scientist. As early as 1886, he saw that logical operations could be carried out by electrical switching circuits - the basis of digital computers. During the 1890s he devised existential graphs, a diagrammatic notation for the predicate calculus. John F. Sowa's conceptual graphs and Sun-Joo Shin's diagrammatic reasoning are based on existential graphs. In computer science, the relational model for databases was developed with Peircean ideas by Edgar F. Codd.

  • Alonzo Church

    Alonzo Church was an American mathematician, computer scientist, logician, and philosopher who made major contributions to mathematical logic and the foundations of theoretical computer science. Alongside his doctoral student Alan Turing, Church is considered one of the founders of computer science. He invented the lambda calculus which influenced the design of Lisp and functional programming languages.

  • John F. Sowa

    John F. Sowa is an American computer scientist, an expert in artificial intelligence and database query linked to natural languages. His work combines ideas from numerous disciplines and eras such as Aristotle, Alfred North Whitehead and Islamic scholar Ibn Taymiyyah. Sowa invented conceptual graphs, a graphic notation for logic and natural language, based on the structures in semantic networks and on the existential graphs of Charles S. Peirce. This presentation is based on Sowa presentations.

  • Tim Berners‒Lee

    Timothy Berners-Lee is an English computer scientist who devised and implemented the first Web browser and Web server. In 1999 Berners-Lee defined linked data - a formal description of concepts, terms, and relationships within a given knowledge domain. To facilitate linked data, Berners-Lee proposes enabling technologies such as: Resource Description Framework (RDF); N-Triples, a format for storing and transmitting data; SPARQL, an RDF query language; and Web Ontology Language (OWL), a family of knowledge representation languages.

  • Philip Johnson‒Laird

    Philip Johnson‒Laird is an expert on human cognition and the psychology of language, thinking and reasoning. He is a developer of the mental model theory of reasoning. Johnson‒Laird has shown that people reason by constructing a set of mental models of possibilities, selecting commonalities, and choosing among them.

  • Antonio Damasio

    Antonio Damasio is a Portuguese-American neurologist and neuroscientist. As a researcher, his main interest is the neurobiology of the mind, especially neural systems which subserve memory, language, emotion, and decision-making. His research has helped to elucidate the neural basis for the emotions and has shown that emotions play a central role in social cognition and decision-making.
